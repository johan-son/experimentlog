Aim

To create an interactive story on a digital platform.


Process

01. Script any dialogue or important text.
02. Briefly create the imagery — either through drawing it or running an image through a dithering filter.
03. Assemble all parts in their respective programs, ensuring an interactive looping flow; Twine/SugarCube for Lord Enma and Figma for Welcome [Back] to Samsara (WBTS).

Reflection

These experiments were a test in creating a choose-your-own-adventure-adjacent text-based interactive story in Twine/SugarCube and Figma respectively. I ended up with short, looping, linear stories about death and reincarnation. For Lord Enma, having a basic knowledge of HTML/CSS already helped a lot with the absolute basics of styling the interactive story on Twine, but I found I was somewhat limited with creating more visual stories with images. As for WBTS, it was a tedious process working on Figma with an interactive project that was already quite long — it started to feel more like a scriptwriting project rather than visual experimentation. In the end, I was still really drawn to the idea of creating a story with a world and plot of some kind, but I decided that choose-your-own-adventure style stories weren’t that enjoyable or effective to make when the primary focus is the writing (which I struggled with when doing these experiments). I also just wanted to do something less serious and more playful moving on from these.

Arenyeka, O. (2025, May 8). What Is a Website Good For? Are.na. https://www.are.na/editorial/what-is-a-website-good-for

Grief Found Me is an interactive web-poem created by Omayeli Arenyeka, an artist and technologist from Nigeria with a degree in Computer Science, Art and Design from NYU. Her article What Is a Website Good for? (published 8 May) focuses on website affordances for poetry and writing, highlighting her web-poem as a work where the qualities of a website enhance the reading experience.
This interactive web-poem and article was an interesting example of interactive non-linear storytelling that gives viewers agency in how they want to read the writing. I explored non-linear storytelling (which was actually just linear in the end) by experimenting with making text-based choose-your-own-adventure style stories on Twine and Figma.
Aim

To visualise a sense of “place” in a digital space.


Process

01. Write up personalised letters to fifteen of my closest online friends.
02. Draw up little “houses” and street roads.
03. Assemble all parts on a website.

Reflection

This was a fun opportunity for me to start getting back into drawing through creating the doodle elements of the sites. I had been thinking about the virtual space as a “place” and how webmasters might make their websites more “placey” with elements that resemble real-world navigation and locations. The intention was to visualise the online space where I met my friends as a fictional location, hence the name “Oomf Street”. This was also a fun opportunity to bring in playfulness. My only gripe with the form (ie. website) was that this website would require more optimisation for better viewing on different screen sizes — I didn’t really want to focus on that, so moving forward from this experiment I wanted to stop doing web coding things and focus more on I enjoyed most about this experiment; the little drawings.

Davis, L. (2025, November 26). The Motherboard Ascension Network. Mmm.page. https://themotherboard.mmm.page/

Motherboard Ascension Network is a 2025 web-based project by mixed-media artist Luna Davis. The project is set around a fictional cult and its AI leader. The Motherboard Ascension Network focuses on discussions of AI, imploring viewers to consider our goals with AI and our willingness to let corporations into our lives to achieve these goals.
The site was made to emulate ‘old web’ cult websites — making me think of the heavensgate.com cult website, with its kitschy and skeuomorphic elements. A part of the site incorporates real photographs of building interiors to emulate a more real sense of “place”. I wanted to emulate the same sense of navigation so I mapped out my online friends on a “street” where it can be navigated down like a real street.
Aim

To reignite my passion for illustration by visualising anything from an existing narrative as a series of drawings.


Process

01. Find text — in this case, the 9 levels of Inferno written in Dante’s Divine Comedy
02. Draw something based on each of the 9 levels.
03. Collate all the drawings.

Reflection

Originally, this was supposed to be a collection of cards in matchbox packaging — this part was never done. This experiment let me step away from more technical side of things and just allowed me to draw whatever I felt like matched the prompts. I wasn’t concerned with what story was being told through the illustrations, however I did draw them using characters I’ve previously made for my own personal projects. From this experiment, I realised that I really just want to take a more playful and illustrative approach to storytelling; something more akin to a graphic novel.

Aim

To combine analogue creation with digital qualities.


Process

01. Sculpt scene elements and characters out of clay, and photograph live models.
02. Edit the elements in Photoshop
03. Composite everything in After Effects

Reflection

I had recently played Hylics, a surreal roleplaying video game by Mason Lindroth that uses clay stop-motion animation for all the characters in the game. This intrigued me, leading me to try experiment with clay stop-motion, and then heavily editing the elements to appear obviously digital. This was a really interesting experiment in combining analogue and digital — something I had been wanting to do from the beginning. This was a way to elevate the surreal world being built. I also worked on a second video after the Fish God one; Shoebox, using a live human model and a box head I made as the second character. Without realising it, I was already building a world and telling a story. With these experiments, I want to find a way to continue expanding on this world, and consider if this series of animations can be shown on a website.
Also, I keep making things that revolve around an afterlife or limbo/purgatory, which is like a type of built world as well. Perhaps something to explore further...

Lindroth, M. (2015). Hylics [Video game Hylics]. ‌

Hylics, released in 2015, is a role-playing video game developed by Mason Lindroth. The game depicts a surreal world, using clay stop-motion animation for the character and worldbuilding elements, and random text generation for character dialogue.
While the turn-based gameplay itself doesn’t engage me, Hylics is an interesting example of surreal worldbuilding with the analogue-digital fusion and dialogue randomisation to match. I wanted to consider how the imperfection of analogue materials (in this case, hand-sculpted clay models) comes into play when worldbuilding an imperfect, weird, or surreal world. This directly influenced my own experimentation with heavily digitally treated claymation (and subsequently, using photographs of live human models) to make something that’s visually weird. Doing multiple of these animations eventually builds a world and story.
